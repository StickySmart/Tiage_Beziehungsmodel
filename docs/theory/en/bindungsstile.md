# Attachment Styles in the Tiage Model

> *Attachment theory as tie-breaker and reflection dimension*
> **Sources:** John Bowlby, Mary Ainsworth — Attachment Theory (1960–1980)

---

## What are attachment styles?

Attachment styles describe **deeply rooted relationship patterns** formed in childhood through the relationship with primary caregivers. They shape all of our subsequent relationship behavior.

Developed by **John Bowlby** (Attachment Theory, 1969) and empirically elaborated by **Mary Ainsworth** (Strange Situation, 1978).

---

## The Four Attachment Styles

### Securely attached

> *"I trust you. I trust myself. We'll find a solution."*

People with a secure attachment style can **balance closeness and distance well**. They seek connection without fear of abandonment and can be alone without falling apart.

- Origin: Consistent, sensitive caregivers in childhood
- Characteristics: Stable self-perception, trust in relationships, capacity for conflict
- In the Tiage model: Neutral baseline — no amplifier, no dampener

### Anxiously attached

> *"Are you leaving me? Do you still love me?"*

**Strong need for closeness and reassurance. Fear of rejection.** The smallest signals of distance are interpreted as threat.

- Origin: Inconsistent caregivers (sometimes present, sometimes not)
- Characteristics: Clinging, excessive need for reassurance, intense jealousy
- In the Tiage model: Increased friction with avoidant partners

### Avoidantly attached

> *"I need space. Feelings feel like loss of control."*

**Distance is sought; closeness can feel overwhelming.** Emotional independence as self-protection.

- Origin: Rejecting or emotionally unavailable caregivers
- Characteristics: Withdrawal during conflict, difficulty with vulnerability, overvaluation of self-sufficiency
- In the Tiage model: Mismatch with anxious style (approach-avoidance dynamic)

### Disorganized / mixed

> *"Come here — go away!"*

**An oscillation between longing for closeness and retreat.** Often connected to early trauma. The attachment partner is simultaneously a source of comfort and threat.

- Origin: Traumatic or frightening experiences with caregivers
- Characteristics: Unpredictability, chaotic behavior in conflicts, intense relationships
- In the Tiage model: Heightened need for consistency and patience from partner

## What Commitment Can Be Today

Attachment theory provides the background for a modern understanding of commitment that the Tiage model implicitly endorses:

| Not | But |
|-----|-----|
| Possession (*"You belong to me"*) | **Reliability** (*"I'm here even when it's difficult"*) |
| Control (*"I decide for the other"*) | **Emotional presence** (*"I show up with my feelings"*) |
| Restriction (*"Freedom and commitment are mutually exclusive"*) | **Responsibility for impact** (*"I'm aware of how I touch you"*) |

> *Even open relationships need commitment — otherwise they become emotionally arbitrary and hollow.*

---

## The Price of Non-Commitment

Non-commitment protects from acute pain — and simultaneously prevents genuine emotional depth.

| Symptom | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Emotional ambiguity** | Constant guessing: What does this mean? Where do we stand? |
| **Inner waiting loops** | The feeling of being in limbo — neither forward nor back |
| **Low-level jealousy** | Because there's no exclusivity, but feelings arise anyway |
| **Self-worth questions** | *"Am I not enough for more?"* — a tormenting question |
| **Blocked needs** | *"I want more, but I'm not allowed to ask for it"* |

---

## Rupture & Repair

Research shows: What makes relationships strong is not the absence of conflict, but the **capacity to repair after ruptures**.

> *Every conflict is a "Rupture." The strength of a relationship shows in the "Repair" — how quickly and fully you find your way back.*

---

## Earned Security

Attachment styles are **not fixed**. "Earned Security" develops through:

1. Conscious reflection on one's own patterns
2. Healing relationship experiences with consistent partners
3. Therapeutic work
4. The Tiage model itself as a reflection tool: *"What do I actually need?"*
